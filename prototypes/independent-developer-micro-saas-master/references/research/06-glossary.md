## Glossary

### TIER 1 — Core Must-Know Terms

---

#### 1. MRR (Monthly Recurring Revenue)

- **Full form**: Monthly Recurring Revenue
- **Definition**: The predictable, normalized revenue a SaaS business can expect to receive every month from active subscriptions. MRR excludes one-time payments, setup fees, and usage-based overages. evidence: [T06-S002] [T06-S017]
- **Formula**: Sum of (active subscribers × monthly plan price), with annual plans divided by 12.
- **Sub-components**:
  - **New MRR**: Revenue from brand-new customers this month
  - **Expansion MRR**: Additional revenue from existing customers (upgrades, seat additions)
  - **Contraction MRR**: Lost revenue from downgrades
  - **Churned MRR**: Revenue lost from cancellations
  - **Net New MRR** = New + Expansion − Contraction − Churned
- **Outsider-tell**: Saying "my MRR is $10k but my revenue was $15k last month" — mixing recurring and one-time income is a beginner error. MRR is a *rate*, not a ledger total. evidence: [T06-S017]
- **Tier**: 1
- **Language**: EN primary

---

#### 2. ARR (Annual Recurring Revenue)

- **Full form**: Annual Recurring Revenue
- **Definition**: ARR = MRR × 12. The annualized view of recurring subscription revenue. Used more often in reporting and investor conversations than in day-to-day operations for micro-SaaS. evidence: [T06-S002] [T06-S018]
- **Outsider-tell**: Treating ARR as "total revenue for the year" — it's a snapshot metric, not a sum of past receipts.
- **Tier**: 1
- **Language**: EN primary

---

#### 3. LTV / CLTV (Customer Lifetime Value)

- **Full form**: Lifetime Value / Customer Lifetime Value
- **Definition**: The total net revenue expected from a single customer over the entire duration of their relationship with the product. evidence: [T06-S018] [T06-S033]
- **Formula (simple)**: LTV = ARPU / Churn Rate (monthly)
- **Formula (margin-adjusted)**: LTV = (ARPU × Gross Margin) / Churn Rate
- **Key ratio**: LTV:CAC ≥ 3:1 is a common benchmark for healthy unit economics. evidence: [T06-S019]
- **Outsider-tell**: Confusing LTV with total revenue, or ignoring gross margin in the calculation.
- **Tier**: 1

---

#### 4. CAC (Customer Acquisition Cost)

- **Full form**: Customer Acquisition Cost
- **Definition**: Total sales and marketing spend divided by the number of new customers acquired in a given period. evidence: [T06-S018]
- **Formula**: CAC = Total S&M Spend / New Customers Acquired
- **Indie context**: For bootstrapped micro-SaaS, CAC can be near-zero via content/SEO/build-in-public, which radically changes unit economics vs. funded SaaS.
- **Outsider-tell**: Forgetting to include founder time cost in CAC calculation; treating organic traffic as "free."
- **Tier**: 1

---

#### 5. Churn Rate

- **Full form**: Customer Churn Rate / Revenue Churn Rate
- **Definition**: The percentage of customers or revenue lost in a given period. evidence: [T06-S003] [T06-S018]
- **Two types — critical distinction**:
  - **Gross (Revenue) Churn**: Revenue lost / beginning-of-period MRR. Always negative.
  - **Net Revenue Retention (NRR)**: Accounts for expansion; can exceed 100% if upsell outpaces churn.
- **Formula (gross MRR churn)**: Churned MRR / MRR at start of period
- **Outsider-tell**: Saying "I had 5 cancellations" as if that equals churn rate; not distinguishing gross from net; thinking churn is just "people who left."
- **Tier**: 1

---

#### 6. NRR (Net Revenue Retention)

- **Full form**: Net Revenue Retention (also Net Dollar Retention, NDR)
- **Definition**: Measures what percentage of revenue is retained from existing customers, including expansions and contractions. NRR > 100% means you grow without any new customers. evidence: [T06-S018] [T06-S033]
- **Formula**: (Beginning MRR + Expansion − Contraction − Churn) / Beginning MRR × 100
- **Benchmark**: Elite SaaS NRR > 120%; micro-SaaS targets > 100%.
- **Tier**: 1

---

#### 7. ARPU (Average Revenue Per User)

- **Full form**: Average Revenue Per User
- **Definition**: MRR divided by total active paying customers. evidence: [T06-S018]
- **Variant**: ARPA (Average Revenue Per Account) when billing is per account, not per seat.
- **Outsider-tell**: Including free users in the denominator — ARPU measures paying users only.
- **Tier**: 1

---

#### 8. Expansion Revenue / Expansion MRR

- **Definition**: Additional MRR generated from existing customers via upgrades, add-ons, or seat expansion. The most capital-efficient form of growth. evidence: [T06-S017] [T06-S018]
- **Indie context**: Key signal of PMF maturity — customers voluntarily paying more.
- **Tier**: 1

---

#### 9. Quick Ratio (SaaS Quick Ratio)

- **Definition**: A growth efficiency metric: (New MRR + Expansion MRR) / (Contraction MRR + Churned MRR). A ratio > 4 is strong; < 1 means you're losing more than you're adding. evidence: [T06-S034]
- **Coined by**: Mamoon Hamid at First Round Capital, popularized ~2015. evidence: [T06-S034]
- **Outsider-tell**: Conflating with accounting Quick Ratio (current assets / current liabilities).
- **Tier**: 1

---

#### 10. Burn Rate & Runway

- **Burn Rate**: Monthly net cash outflow when expenses exceed revenue. evidence: [T06-S019]
- **Runway**: Months of operation remaining = Cash / Monthly Burn Rate.
- **Indie context**: Bootstrapped founders typically have very low burn (often < $1k/mo in infrastructure costs); runway is extended by personal savings or existing income.
- **Tier**: 1

---

#### 11. Ramen Profitability

- **Definition**: A startup earns just enough to cover founders' basic living costs (ramen = bare minimum food). Coined by Paul Graham. The milestone that removes existential pressure without requiring outside funding. evidence: [T06-S019]
- **Outsider-tell**: Treating ramen profitability as a goal rather than a floor; confusing it with actual business profitability.
- **Tier**: 1

---

#### 12. Default Alive / Default Dead

- **Definition**: Coined by Paul Graham. A company is "default alive" if, at current growth and burn, it will reach profitability before running out of money — without needing additional funding. "Default dead" is the inverse. evidence: [T06-S019]
- **Formula**: Uses current revenue, growth rate, and expenses to project runway vs. profitability crossover.
- **Tier**: 1

---

#### 13. PMF (Product-Market Fit)

- **Full form**: Product-Market Fit
- **Definition**: The degree to which a product satisfies strong market demand. Marc Andreessen: "being in a good market with a product that can satisfy that market." Sean Ellis test: > 40% of users would be "very disappointed" without the product. evidence: [T06-S019]
- **Indie-specific signals**: Low churn, organic referrals, customers asking for more features rather than leaving.
- **Outsider-tell**: Declaring PMF after a few sales; using "people like it" as the metric.
- **Tier**: 1

---

#### 14. MVP (Minimum Viable Product)

- **Full form**: Minimum Viable Product
- **Definition**: The smallest version of a product that delivers enough value to validate a hypothesis and collect real user feedback. Not "minimum" in quality — minimum in scope. evidence: [T06-S019]
- **Indie context**: Often a landing page + manual fulfillment, a no-code prototype, or a single-feature tool.
- **Outsider-tell**: Building MVP for months before launching; treating MVP as "complete app."
- **Tier**: 1

---

#### 15. Build in Public (BiP)

- **Definition**: The practice of sharing real-time metrics, decisions, failures, and milestones publicly (Twitter/X, newsletters, forums) as a distribution and accountability strategy. evidence: [T06-S003]
- **Distribution mechanism**: Attracts early adopters, journalists, and investors; creates social proof.
- **Risk**: Competitors can monitor your metrics and roadmap.
- **Outsider-tell**: Treating it as mere "marketing" rather than a community-building and accountability system.
- **Tier**: 1

---

#### 16. Scratching Your Own Itch

- **Definition**: Building a product that solves a problem you personally experience. The foundational indie hacker philosophy — you are the domain expert and first customer. evidence: [T06-S003] [T06-S036]
- **Outsider-tell**: Pitching an itch-scratch product to VCs expecting a $1B TAM.
- **Tier**: 1

---

#### 17. Landing Page / Smoke Test

- **Landing page**: A dedicated conversion page that describes a product's value proposition before (or independent of) the product existing.
- **Smoke test**: Measuring real demand by offering a product before building it — if people click "Buy" or enter their email, demand is validated. evidence: [T06-S003]
- **Indie variant**: Collecting a waitlist, charging a deposit, or running a pre-sale as validation.
- **Tier**: 1

---

#### 18. Freemium

- **Definition**: A pricing model where a base product tier is permanently free, with paid tiers unlocking additional features. evidence: [T06-S002] [T06-S033]
- **Indie warning**: Freemium is hard to execute at micro-SaaS scale — high support cost for free users with low conversion. Most indie founders prefer free trial.
- **Conversion benchmark**: 2–5% free-to-paid is considered acceptable; < 1% signals product or positioning problem.
- **Outsider-tell**: Choosing freemium as a default without understanding support cost implications.
- **Tier**: 1

---

#### 19. Free Trial

- **Definition**: Time-limited full or partial access to a paid product before purchase. Two variants: credit-card-required (CCR) vs. no-card. CCR trials have lower volume but higher conversion. evidence: [T06-S033]
- **Tier**: 1

---

#### 20. Paywall

- **Definition**: A gating mechanism that restricts access to features or content behind a payment or subscription. Can be hard (full block) or soft (usage-limited). evidence: [T06-S033]
- **Tier**: 1

---

#### 21. Feature Flag

- **Definition**: A code-level mechanism that enables or disables product features without deployment. Used for A/B testing, staged rollouts, and plan-gating. evidence: [T06-S038]
- **Indie use**: Gating premium features behind subscription tiers.
- **Tier**: 1

---

#### 22. A/B Test

- **Definition**: A controlled experiment comparing two variants (A and B) of a page, price, or feature to determine which drives better conversion outcomes. evidence: [T06-S003]
- **Indie context**: Requires sufficient traffic — micro-SaaS products often lack statistical power for rigorous A/B testing.
- **Tier**: 1

---

#### 23. Indie Hacker

- **Definition**: A person who builds internet businesses (typically SaaS, content products, or tools) independently, usually without VC funding, aiming for sustainable revenue over hypergrowth. Platform: IndieHackers.com (founded 2016 by Courtland Allen, acquired by Stripe). evidence: [T06-S003]
- **Outsider-tell**: Treating "indie hacker" as synonymous with "freelancer" — they build products, not client services.
- **Tier**: 1

---

#### 24. Bootstrapped

- **Definition**: Building a business without external equity investment, funded entirely by revenue, savings, or small loans. evidence: [T06-S036] [T06-S021]
- **Spectrum**: Pre-revenue bootstrapped → ramen profitable → sustainably profitable.
- **Outsider-tell**: Calling a company "bootstrapped" after taking a bank loan; confusing bootstrapped with unfunded.
- **Tier**: 1

---

#### 25. Solopreneur / One-Person Business

- **Definition**: A business operator who runs the entire operation alone — no co-founders, no employees. Evidence: [T06-S015]
- **Distinction from freelancer**: Solopreneurs build scalable systems (SaaS, content, courses); freelancers trade time for money.
- **Tier**: 1

---

#### 26. Micro-SaaS

- **Definition**: A small, focused SaaS product built and operated by an individual or tiny team (≤ 3), targeting a narrow niche with a specific problem. Coined by Tyler Tringas, ~2013. evidence: [T06-S015]
- **Characteristics**: Low operational cost, high profit margin, no VC funding, often < $50k MRR, sustainable over hypergrowth.
- **Outsider-tell**: Calling any small SaaS "micro-SaaS" — the term implies intentional constraint and niche focus, not just company size.
- **Tier**: 1

---

#### 27. Stair-Step Approach

- **Definition**: A bootstrapping philosophy by Rob Walling. Start with a one-time-sale product (template, plugin) → graduate to low-MRR SaaS → then higher-commitment SaaS. Each step builds skills and reduces risk. evidence: [T06-S036]
- **Outsider-tell**: Jumping straight to a complex SaaS without first proving you can ship and sell.
- **Tier**: 1

---

#### 28. AppSumo LTD (Lifetime Deal)

- **Definition**: A one-time-payment deal for lifetime access to a SaaS product, sold through AppSumo marketplace. evidence: [T06-S026]
- **Indie risk**: LTD buyers have low willingness-to-pay long-term; high support burden; cash is front-loaded but future obligation is permanent. Most indie advisors recommend using LTD as a launch spike, not a business model.
- **Outsider-tell**: Treating AppSumo #1 or a successful LTD campaign as validation of a sustainable SaaS business model.
- **Tier**: 1

---

#### 29. Product Hunt Launch

- **Definition**: Submitting a product to Product Hunt for community discovery. A top-ranked launch (#1 Product of the Day) drives short-term traffic spikes. evidence: [T06-S025]
- **Reality check**: Product Hunt #1 rarely correlates with revenue. Most indie founders report < $1k in revenue from a PH launch. It signals social proof, not distribution.
- **Outsider-tell**: Treating a PH launch as a distribution strategy or PMF signal.
- **Tier**: 1

---

#### 30. Distribution Moat

- **Definition**: A sustainable competitive advantage derived from how a product reaches its customers — not from the product itself. SEO, audience, community, integrations, or word-of-mouth that competitors cannot easily replicate. evidence: [T06-S027]
- **Indie importance**: At micro-SaaS scale, product differentiation is fragile; distribution is the durable advantage.
- **Tier**: 1

---

#### 31. Wrapper (AI Wrapper)

- **Definition**: A SaaS product built primarily by calling a third-party AI API (e.g., OpenAI, Anthropic) and adding a UI, workflow, or niche-specific framing. evidence: [T06-S003]
- **Debate**: Critics argue wrappers have no moat (provider can compete directly or cut off access). Proponents note distribution and niche positioning create real value.
- **Outsider-tell**: Assuming any AI wrapper is a "real product" without analyzing lock-in and switching costs.
- **Tier**: 1

---

#### 32. 1000 True Fans

- **Definition**: Kevin Kelly's 2008 essay thesis: a creator needs only 1,000 people who will buy anything you produce to sustain a living. For SaaS: 1,000 paying customers × $X ARPU = sustainability. evidence: [T06-S029]
- **Indie application**: Focus on depth of engagement with a small audience, not breadth.
- **Tier**: 1

---

#### 33. Audience-First

- **Definition**: A product development philosophy where you build an audience (newsletter, Twitter following, YouTube channel) before or alongside building a product. Distribution precedes product. evidence: [T06-S027]
- **Risk**: Audience building is slow; products built for audience expectations may not solve real pain.
- **Tier**: 1

---

#### 34. Boring Technology

- **Definition**: Choosing mature, well-understood technology stacks over novel/trendy alternatives to reduce operational risk and cognitive overhead. Coined by Dan McKinley (Etsy). In indie context: use boring tech to ship faster and maintain alone. evidence: [T06-S038]
- **Tier**: 1

---

#### 35. Ship Fast

- **Definition**: A cultural value prioritizing rapid deployment and iteration over exhaustive pre-launch preparation. "Done is better than perfect." Derived from Facebook's early "move fast" culture, adapted for indie context. evidence: [T06-S019]
- **Tension**: Shipping fast requires technical debt tolerance; micro-SaaS founders often have no team to manage that debt.
- **Tier**: 1

---

### TIER 2 — Technical Architecture Terms

---

#### 36. Monolith-First

- **Definition**: The pattern of starting with a single deployable application (monolith) before considering microservices. Martin Fowler's recommendation; essential for solo developers who cannot operationally manage distributed systems. evidence: [T06-S038]
- **Tier**: 2

---

#### 37. Serverless

- **Definition**: A cloud execution model where the provider manages infrastructure, scaling, and provisioning. Developers deploy functions; billing is per invocation. evidence: [T06-S038]
- **Examples**: AWS Lambda, Cloudflare Workers, Vercel Functions.
- **Indie benefit**: Eliminates server management; near-zero idle cost.
- **Tier**: 2

---

#### 38. Edge Functions

- **Definition**: Serverless functions executed at CDN edge nodes geographically close to the user, reducing latency. evidence: [T06-S039]
- **Examples**: Cloudflare Workers, Vercel Edge Functions.
- **Tier**: 2

---

#### 39. BaaS (Backend as a Service)

- **Full form**: Backend as a Service
- **Definition**: A platform that provides pre-built backend infrastructure (database, authentication, storage, real-time) via SDK, eliminating need to build/manage servers. evidence: [T06-S040]
- **Examples**: Firebase (Google), Supabase, Pocketbase.
- **Tier**: 2

---

#### 40. Auth as a Service

- **Definition**: Third-party authentication infrastructure (login, OAuth, MFA, session management) delivered as a managed service. evidence: [T06-S040]
- **Examples**: Auth0, Clerk, Supabase Auth.
- **Indie benefit**: Eliminates security liability of rolling your own auth.
- **Tier**: 2

---

#### 41. Payment Gateway

- **Definition**: Infrastructure that processes payment transactions between customer and merchant, handling card data, fraud detection, and settlement. evidence: [T06-S012]
- **Examples**: Stripe, Paddle, LemonSqueezy.
- **Tier**: 2

---

#### 42. Webhook

- **Definition**: An HTTP callback triggered by an event in a third-party system, sending real-time data to a specified endpoint. Used for subscription lifecycle events (payment succeeded, subscription cancelled). evidence: [T06-S012]
- **Critical for SaaS**: Stripe webhooks trigger access grants/revocations.
- **Tier**: 2

---

#### 43. Multi-tenant

- **Definition**: A single instance of a software application serving multiple customers (tenants), with data isolation between them. evidence: [T06-S018]
- **Vs. single-tenant**: SaaS is typically multi-tenant; enterprise deals sometimes demand single-tenant for compliance.
- **Tier**: 2

---

#### 44. SSR / SSG / ISR

- **Full forms**: Server-Side Rendering / Static Site Generation / Incremental Static Regeneration
- **Definitions**: Web rendering strategies determining when HTML is generated (per-request vs. build-time vs. hybrid). evidence: [T06-S039]
- **Indie use**: SSG for marketing pages (SEO, fast); SSR for dynamic app; ISR for content that changes infrequently.
- **Tier**: 2

---

#### 45. Jamstack

- **Definition**: Architecture pattern: JavaScript + APIs + Markup. Decouples frontend (static) from backend (APIs). Popularized by Netlify/Vercel ecosystem. evidence: [T06-S039]
- **Tier**: 2

---

#### 46. API-First

- **Definition**: Designing a product's API as the primary interface, with UI as a secondary consumer. Enables integrations and B2B embedding from day one. evidence: [T06-S040]
- **Tier**: 2

---

#### 47. Headless CMS

- **Definition**: A content management system that provides content via API, decoupled from frontend presentation. evidence: [T06-S039]
- **Examples**: Contentful, Sanity, Strapi.
- **Tier**: 2

---

### TIER 2 — Pricing & Business Model Terms

---

#### 48. Contraction MRR

- **Definition**: Revenue reduction from existing customers who downgrade their plan without fully cancelling. Part of gross churn calculation. evidence: [T06-S017] [T06-S018]
- **Tier**: 2

---

#### 49. Payback Period

- **Definition**: Time to recoup CAC from a customer's gross profit contribution. Payback < 12 months is healthy for bootstrapped SaaS; < 6 months is excellent. evidence: [T06-S033]
- **Tier**: 2

---

#### 50. Value Metric / Usage-Based Pricing

- **Definition**: Pricing tied to the unit of value a customer consumes (e.g., per seat, per API call, per email sent). Aligns price with value delivered. evidence: [T06-S016] [T06-S033]
- **Patio11 note**: The pricing axis you choose is a strategic decision — choose a metric customers understand and accept. evidence: [T06-S016]
- **Tier**: 2

---

#### 51. Annual Plan (Prepaid Annual)

- **Definition**: Customer pays 12 months upfront, typically at 2-month discount. Reduces churn probability; improves cash flow. evidence: [T06-S002]
- **Accounting note**: Annual plan revenue is recognized monthly (deferred revenue); do not count the full prepayment as MRR.
- **Tier**: 2

### TIER 1–2 — Chinese-Ecosystem Terms

---

#### 52. 出海 (Chūhǎi)

- **Definition**: Literally "going overseas." In tech context: Chinese indie developers or companies targeting international markets, typically building in English, using Stripe, and listed on global platforms. evidence: [T06-S003]
- **Distinction**: 出海 indie devs often face additional regulatory complexity (foreign payment processing, PIPL compliance, data residency).
- **Tier**: 1 (zh-CN context)

#### 53. 独立开发者 (Dúlì Kāifāzhě)

- **Definition**: Literally "independent developer." The zh-CN equivalent of "indie developer" or "indie hacker." Encompasses solopreneurs building apps, SaaS, and digital products. evidence: [T06-S003]
- **Tier**: 1 (zh-CN)

---

#### 54. 小而美 (Xiǎo ér měi)

- **Definition**: Literally "small but beautiful." Philosophy of building focused, elegant products rather than feature-bloated ones. Equivalent to micro-SaaS ethos in Chinese indie culture.
- **Tier**: 1 (zh-CN)

---

#### 55. 一人公司 (Yī rén gōngsī)

- **Definition**: Literally "one-person company." Equivalent of solopreneur/one-person business. In zh-CN context, often registered as an individual business (个体工商户) or via Hong Kong company.
- **Tier**: 1 (zh-CN)

---

#### 56. 副业 (Fùyè)

- **Definition**: Literally "side business/side hustle." A secondary income project alongside a primary job. Many zh-CN indie developers start with 副业 before going full-time. Equivalent of "side project."
- **Tier**: 1 (zh-CN)

---

#### 57. 数字游民 (Shùzì Yóumín)

- **Definition**: Digital nomad. Remote-working lifestyle enabled by location-independent online income. High cultural cachet in zh-CN indie communities. evidence: [T06-S003]
- **Tier**: 2 (zh-CN)

---

#### 58. 被动收入 (Bèidòng shōurù)

- **Definition**: Passive income. A widely misunderstood aspiration in zh-CN indie circles. SaaS requires continuous maintenance, support, and iteration — it is not truly passive. "Passive income" framing attracts unrealistic expectations.
- **Outsider-tell**: Describing SaaS as "passive income" reveals unfamiliarity with actual operational requirements.
- **Tier**: 1 (zh-CN)

---

#### 59. 知识付费 (Zhīshi fùfèi)

- **Definition**: "Knowledge monetization" — paid online courses, newsletters, templates, and educational content. A major revenue category for zh-CN indie creators. Distinct from SaaS (software subscription).
- **Tier**: 2 (zh-CN)

---

#### 60. 小报童 (Xiǎo Bàotóng)

- **Definition**: A zh-CN newsletter/content subscription platform (similar to Substack). Used by indie creators for paid newsletter monetization. evidence: [T06-S003]
- **Tier**: 2 (zh-CN)

---

#### 61. 爱发电 (Ài Fādiàn)

- **Definition**: A zh-CN crowdfunding/patronage platform (similar to Patreon/Ko-fi). Enables one-time or recurring support for indie creators and developers.
- **Tier**: 2 (zh-CN)

---

#### 62. ICP 备案 (ICP Bèi'àn)

- **Definition**: Internet Content Provider filing. Mandatory registration with China's Ministry of Industry and Information Technology (MIIT) for any website hosted in mainland China. Without ICP 备案, the site can be blocked. evidence: [T06-S013]
- **Types**: ICP 经营性备案 (commercial) vs. ICP 非经营性备案 (non-commercial).
- **Processing time**: 20–30 business days typically.
- **Tier**: 1 (zh-CN)

### Standards, Regulations & Certifications

---

#### 63. GDPR (General Data Protection Regulation)

- **Full form**: General Data Protection Regulation
- **Jurisdiction**: European Union
- **Effective date**: 2018-05-25 evidence: [T06-S005]
- **Scope**: Applies to any product processing personal data of EU residents, regardless of where the company is incorporated.
- **Key obligations for indie SaaS**: Privacy policy, lawful basis for processing, data subject rights (access, deletion, portability), data breach notification within 72 hours.
- **Penalties**: Up to €20M or 4% of global annual turnover.
- **Outsider-tell**: Thinking GDPR only applies to EU-registered companies.
- **Tier**: 1 (regulatory)
- **Standard body**: European Parliament / Council of the EU

---

#### 64. CCPA / CPRA (California Consumer Privacy Act / California Privacy Rights Act)

- **Jurisdiction**: California, USA
- **Effective dates**: CCPA 2020-01-01; CPRA (amendment) 2023-01-01 evidence: [T06-S006]
- **Scope**: Applies to for-profit businesses collecting CA residents' personal data meeting certain thresholds (> $25M revenue OR > 100k consumers' data OR > 50% revenue from selling data).
- **Key rights**: Right to know, right to delete, right to opt-out of data sale.
- **Indie note**: Most micro-SaaS fall below CCPA thresholds, but privacy policy compliance is still best practice.
- **Tier**: 1 (regulatory)

---

#### 65. PCI DSS

- **Full form**: Payment Card Industry Data Security Standard
- **Standard body**: PCI Security Standards Council evidence: [T06-S007]
- **Current version**: PCI DSS v4.0 (released March 2022, mandatory compliance from March 2024)
- **Scope**: Any entity storing, processing, or transmitting cardholder data.
- **Indie implication**: Using Stripe/Paddle/LemonSqueezy as payment infrastructure allows delegation of PCI scope — never store raw card numbers.
- **Levels**: SAQ A (redirect/hosted), SAQ D (full processing) — most micro-SaaS qualify for SAQ A.
- **Tier**: 1 (standard)

---

#### 66. SOC 2 (System and Organization Controls 2)

- **Full form**: Service Organization Control Type 2
- **Standard body**: AICPA (American Institute of CPAs) evidence: [T06-S008]
- **Purpose**: Audit framework evaluating security, availability, processing integrity, confidentiality, and privacy controls for cloud service providers.
- **Types**: SOC 2 Type I (point-in-time design assessment); SOC 2 Type II (operational effectiveness over 6–12 months).
- **Indie context**: Required by enterprise B2B customers; typically not achieved until > $100k ARR. Cost: $15k–$50k for audit. Startups use Vanta/Drata for compliance automation.
- **Tier**: 2 (standard — adjacent)

---

#### 67. DMCA (Digital Millennium Copyright Act)

- **Full form**: Digital Millennium Copyright Act
- **Jurisdiction**: United States
- **Effective date**: 1998-10-28 evidence: [T06-S009] [T06-S020]
- **Relevant provisions**: § 512 Safe Harbor — platforms that respond promptly to takedown notices are not liable for user-uploaded infringing content.
- **Indie relevance**: If your product hosts user content, implement a DMCA takedown process.
- **Tier**: 2 (regulatory)

---

#### 68. Apple App Store Review Guidelines

- **Standard body**: Apple Inc. evidence: [T06-S010]
- **Current version**: Living document, updated continuously (last major revision 2024)
- **Key sections for indie SaaS**: § 3.1 (In-App Purchase requirement for digital goods), § 3.1.3 (reader apps), Anti-steering provisions (post-Epic v. Apple consent decree modifications, 2024).
- **Indie impact**: 30% commission (15% for < $1M ARR via Small Business Program) on in-app purchases. Physical goods and services exempt.
- **Tier**: 1 (platform standard)

---

#### 69. Google Play Developer Distribution Agreement / Content Policy

- **Standard body**: Google LLC evidence: [T06-S011]
- **Current version**: Living document
- **Parallel to App Store**: Similar IAP requirements, commission structure, and content restrictions.
- **Tier**: 1 (platform standard)

---

#### 70. Stripe Terms of Service (Stripe Services Agreement)

- **Standard body**: Stripe, Inc. evidence: [T06-S012]
- **Key provisions for indie SaaS**: Acceptable use policy (prohibited business categories), payout timing, reserve policies, account termination risks.
- **Indie risk**: Stripe can freeze accounts with limited notice. High chargeback rate (> 1%) triggers risk review. Some business categories (firearms, adult, certain crypto) are prohibited.
- **Tier**: 1 (platform standard)

---

#### 71. 中国网络安全法 (Cybersecurity Law of the PRC)

- **Jurisdiction**: People's Republic of China
- **Effective date**: 2017-06-01 evidence: [T06-S031]
- **Key obligations**: Data localization for certain data types, network security multi-level protection scheme (MLPS/等级保护), real-name registration.
- **Tier**: 1 (regulatory, zh-CN)

---

#### 72. 中国数据安全法 (Data Security Law of the PRC)

- **Jurisdiction**: People's Republic of China
- **Effective date**: 2021-09-01 evidence: [T06-S031]
- **Key obligations**: Data classification, important data protection, cross-border data transfer restrictions.
- **Tier**: 1 (regulatory, zh-CN)

---

#### 73. 个人信息保护法 (PIPL — Personal Information Protection Law)

- **Full form**: 个人信息保护法 (Personal Information Protection Law)
- **Jurisdiction**: People's Republic of China
- **Effective date**: 2021-11-01 evidence: [T06-S030]
- **Scope**: Applies to processing personal information of individuals in China, including by overseas entities.
- **Key provisions**: Consent requirements, cross-border transfer rules (standard contracts or security assessments), data subject rights.
- **Analog**: China's GDPR equivalent.
- **Tier**: 1 (regulatory, zh-CN)

---

#### 74. LLC / Ltd / GmbH — Entity Structures

- **LLC (Limited Liability Company)**: US structure. Pass-through taxation, limited liability. Delaware LLC popular for indie SaaS (flexible, VC-acceptable). evidence: [T06-S022]
- **Ltd (Private Limited Company)**: UK/Commonwealth structure. Equivalent liability protection. UK Ltd popular for 出海 developers due to low formation cost (~£12). evidence: [T06-S023]
- **GmbH (Gesellschaft mit beschränkter Haftung)**: German equivalent. Higher capitalization requirement (€25k). evidence: [T06-S024]
- **Hong Kong Ltd**: Popular for zh-CN indie developers — access to international banking, Stripe, without PRC data localization requirements.
- **Tier**: 2 (legal structure)

### Additional Tier 1–2 Terms

---

#### 79. Side Project

- **Definition**: A project worked on outside of primary employment, often evenings/weekends. The typical origin story for most indie SaaS products. Carries legal risk if employment contract has IP assignment clauses.
- **Outsider-tell**: Treating "side project" and "indie business" as synonymous — side project is a development stage, not a business model.
- **Tier**: 1

---

#### 80. Small Bets

- **Definition**: A portfolio strategy for indie developers — instead of committing fully to one product, make multiple small, cheap experiments. Associated with Daniel Vassallo. Hedges against single-product failure.
- **Tension**: Contradicts "go deep" advice; portfolio approach can prevent focus.
- **Tier**: 2

---

#### 81. Waitlist

- **Definition**: An email collection mechanism for a pre-launch product, used to gauge demand and build a launch audience. evidence: [T06-S003]
- **Viral mechanics**: Referral-based waitlist (e.g., early access for sharing) amplifies list growth.
- **Tier**: 1

---

#### 82. Beta Launch

- **Definition**: A limited release to a subset of users (beta testers) before full public availability. Gathers feedback, identifies bugs, and builds initial social proof. evidence: [T06-S003]
- **Tier**: 1

### 行业表达 DNA (直接素材)

**Characteristic vocabulary patterns in EN indie hacker discourse:**
- Heavy use of "$X MRR" as primary status signal ("just hit $5k MRR")
- "Ship" as a verb for deployment ("shipped v2 today")
- "Churn" as both noun and verb
- "@mention builds in public" as social currency
- "Found PMF" used loosely, often prematurely
- Revenue milestones shared publicly: "crossed $10k MRR", "hit ramen profitability"
- "Bootstrapped, profitable, and growing" as the trifecta status phrase
- "X → Y" formatting for transitions ("side project → $8k MRR")

**Characteristic vocabulary in zh-CN 独立开发者 discourse:**
- 月收入 / 月流水 (monthly revenue) rather than MRR
- 出海了！ as celebratory milestone

- 副业月入xxx 为诱导性标题 (clickbait pattern)
- 做了一个工具 / 做了一个产品 — humble launch framing

4. Understanding that Stripe reserves can lock funds for 30–120 days in high-churn categories

9. Referencing MicroConf as the community, not generic "startup community"
10. Saying "default alive" calculation rather than just "runway"
11. Knowing that annual plan bookings must be amortized monthly for MRR accuracy
12. Distinguishing ICP 经营性 vs. 非经营性 备案 categories
